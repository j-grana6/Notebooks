Pitcher's performance against the same team¶

(or, fool me once shame on you, fool me twice, shame on me).¶

The basic idea is that when hitters see pitchers often, they begin to "understand" the pitcher. However, hitters also have a short memory. That means that for hitters to gain anything from seeing a pitcher multiple times, there can't be a large chunk of time between appearances. To see if this is the case we follow a simple procedure :

  1. Determine a metric that measures the pitchers performance
  2. Choose a time limit between matchups
  3. Specify the pitchers to consider
  4. Compute the difference of the metric in the first start and the second start
  5. Check if the differenc is due to randomness or is a significant difference

Experiment 1:¶

  1. ERA
  2. 1 Month
  3. All pitchers with at least 15 Starts

Some Code (can be skipped of course)¶

The way the code works is that I have the 2014 pitching values tables saved as a text file (this could easily be scraped directly from baseball references). I then extract baseball reference's unique player id. I know that the game logs for 2014 are at URL "http://www.baseball-reference.com/players/gl.cgi?id={UNIQUEPLAYERID}&t=p&year=2014".

I then use urllib2 to navigate to that website and get the game logs table. From here I can loop through games and compute the difference in the metric for any 2 games that are close enough.

Results¶

All in all, we have 118 pitchers and 318 games that qualify (i.e. there are 318 games where a pitcher faced the same team twice within a month. Here are the results

  • The average difference in ERA from the first start to the second start is $.07$. That is, on average the ERA in the second start is .07 higher.
  • The median ERA difference is 0. This means that 50% of the time, the pitcher's ERA rises and 50% of the time it falls in the second outing against the same team.
  • This increase is not statistically significant. That is, we cannot say with certainty the .07 rise in ERA is actually due to some factor rather then pure randomness.
  • The confidence interval is [-.05, .2032] (this was done via empirical likelihood)
  • So all statistics aside, even if the .07 was statistically significant, it is so small that it is of little practical use
  • We can look at the histogram and see that the distribution is centered around zero. It is ever so slightly skewed to the right,

Main result¶

Given that any ERA change is subtle and that changing the time interval gives contradicting results, we can say that pitchers do not do worse (or better) when they face the same team in a short time interval.

Looking at other statistics¶

Maybe ERA is too general of a measure and we need to look at some other measures. Let's look at:

  • Innings pitched
  • Strikeout rate (number of strikeouts/number of batters faced)
  • Opponent batting average

I will present the mean difference, the confidence interval and a histogram. Note that a difference is considered statistically significant if the interval does not contain 0.

Summary¶

In general, we do not see any statistically or practically significant difference in a pitcher's performance when he faces the same team in a short time span. The only exception is opponent batting average. On average, we see a 30 points increase in opponents batting average when they see the same pitcher twice in a short time span.

The interesting question now is why doesn't that increase in batting average induce a higher pitcher ERA? From a fantasy perspective, is the increase in 30 points a significant enough factor when choosing which pitchers to start for the week?
